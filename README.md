INTRODUCTION

Electrostatics and magnetostatics describe electric and magnetic fields under steady, unchanging conditions. Electrostatics focuses on electric charges at rest and the fields they produce, while magnetostatics deals with magnetic fields created by steady currents. The fundamental laws‚Äîsuch as Coulomb‚Äôs law, Gauss‚Äôs law, the Biot‚ÄìSavart law, and Amp√®re‚Äôs law‚Äîprovide the basic tools for understanding how charges and currents interact with their surroundings. These principles form the foundation for many applications in physics and engineering.

Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience forces in the presence of other charged objects. There are two types of charges: positive and negative. Like charges repel each other, while unlike charges attract. Charge is measured in coulombs (C) and is conserved, meaning it cannot be created or destroyed‚Äîonly transferred from one object to another.

Charges exist in two forms:

Point charges: Idealized charges with no physical size.

Distributed charges: Spread over a surface, line, or volume.

Direction of the Force

If the charges have like signs, the force is repulsive.

If the charges have opposite signs, the force is attractive.

The force acts along the straight line connecting the two charges.


Electric Field and Electric Potential
Electric Field (E)

An electric field is a region in space where a charged object experiences a force. It describes how a source charge influences other charges around it. The electric field is defined as the force per unit positive test charge placed at a point.

Gauss‚Äôs Law

Gauss‚Äôs Law is one of Maxwell‚Äôs equations. It relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the charge enclosed by that surface.

Statement

The total electric flux through a closed surface is equal to the charge enclosed divided by the permittivity of free space.

Meaning

Electric flux represents how many electric field lines pass through a surface.

If more charge is inside the surface, more field lines emerge.

Gauss‚Äôs law simplifies electric field calculations for highly symmetric charge distributions.

Dielectrics and Capacitors
1. Dielectrics

A dielectric is an insulating material that does not allow free movement of charges but can be polarized when placed in an electric field.

Examples

Glass, plastic, mica, paper, air, ceramic, polymer films.

Properties of Dielectrics

High electrical resistivity

Supports electric field with very small leakage current

Gets polarized and stores electric energy

Defined by dielectric constant

Polarization

When a dielectric is placed in an electric field, charges inside shift slightly, creating dipoles.
Polarization reduces the effective electric field inside the material.

Electrostatic Applications in Technology

Electrostatics deals with electric charges at rest.
Many modern devices and industrial systems use electrostatic principles for operation, safety, or efficiency.
Below are the major applications:

1. Electrostatic Precipitators (ESP)

Used in power plants, cement factories, and industries to remove dust and smoke particles from exhaust gases.

Gas is passed through high-voltage plates ‚Üí particles become charged ‚Üí collected on oppositely charged plates.

Helps in pollution control.

2. Photocopiers (Xerox Machines)

Works using electrostatic charge patterns on a photoconductive drum.

Light removes charges from bright areas; dark areas retain charge ‚Üí toner (charged powder) sticks ‚Üí transferred to paper ‚Üí heated to fuse.

Fast, reliable document reproduction.

3. Laser Printers

Similar to photocopiers.

A laser writes a charge pattern on a drum.

Toner is attracted to charged areas and transferred to paper to form text/images.

4. Electrostatic Painting / Powder Coating

Charged paint particles are sprayed onto an object with opposite charge.

Paint spreads uniformly, reduces wastage, and increases adhesion.

Used in automobiles, furniture, and appliances.

5. Capacitive Touchscreens

Works based on change in capacitance when a finger touches the screen.

The human body acts as a conductor and disturbs the local electric field.

Used in smartphones, ATMs, and displays.

6. Inkjet Printers

Droplets of ink are charged and deflected by electric fields to place them accurately on paper.

Gives high-resolution printing.

7. Electrostatic Speakers

Use electric fields to move thin membranes back and forth to generate sound.

Provide very clear, high-fidelity audio.

8. Van de Graaff Generator

Uses electrostatic charging to produce very high voltages.

Used in physics laboratories, particle acceleration, and research.

9. Electrostatic Motors

Motors that operate on attraction and repulsion of electrostatically charged plates.

Used in microelectromechanical systems (MEMS).

10. Spray Painting / Agricultural Sprayers

Electrostatic spraying of fertilizers and pesticides ensures even coating and reduces chemical usage.

Summary

Electrostatics and magnetostatics are two major branches of electromagnetism dealing with static (time-independent) fields.

Electrostatics

Deals with electric charges at rest.

Main quantities: Electric field E, Electric potential V, Charge density œÅ.

Field is derived from charges using Coulomb‚Äôs law, Gauss‚Äôs law, and potential relations.

Electric field is conservative (curl E = 0).

Magnetostatics

Deals with steady electric currents (constant in time).

Main quantities: Magnetic field B, Magnetic field intensity H, Current density J.

Field is produced by steady currents using Biot‚ÄìSavart Law and Amp√®re‚Äôs Law.

Magnetic field is solenoidal (div B = 0).

Both fields are part of Maxwell‚Äôs equations under static conditions.
